gQIR

Fast, Vectorized Color-QBP Implementation and The Need for Priors

CS766 Final Project (Spring 2026)

Aryan Garg, Avery Gump
Midterm Report Presentation Code

Recovering high-fidelity, color HDR images from Single Photon Cameras using a fast, vectorized reimplementation of the traditional Color Quanta Burst Photography Algorithm.

Abstract

We present a comprehensive reimplementation of color Quanta Burst Photography (cQBP) that is fast and vectorized. Written in PyTorch, our reimplementation achieves significant speedups while maintaining fidelity through pinned memory transfers, separate CUDA streams for parallelization, and concurrent data transfer and computation. Then we evaluate this implementation on an ongoing ICCV Workshop challenge and also compare with the latest generative SPAD ISP while highlighting the pros and cons of using the generative method. We finally propose a direction that relies on generative priors and produces high dynamic range outputs.

Motivation & Background

The impossible triangle of perception—fast motion, high dynamic range, and low light—has long constrained CMOS imaging. A rapidly emerging sensing paradigm seeks to shatter these limits by imaging at the granularity of individual photons.

Single-Photon Avalanche Diodes (SPADs) capture photons one-by-one, enabling burst photography with extreme motion handling and wide dynamic range. However, raw quanta frames are binary, temporally misaligned, and bayer-mosaicked. Traditional reconstruction pipelines falter under Bernoulli photon statistics, and supervised learning approaches are inherently biased toward high distortion that violates natural image priors.

This creates a clear gap: physics-based ISPs lack perceptual realism, while generative models ignore the underlying photon physics. Our work bridges this divide by first delivering a production-grade, vectorized cQBP engine, then evaluating how far generative priors can carry us—and where they fundamentally break.

Limitations of Generative SPAD ISPs

While gQIR and similar generative methods deliver striking perceptual quality, they suffer from critical limitations rooted in their pretraining data:

  • LDR Pretraining Bias: Models trained on low-dynamic-range (LDR) internet images cannot natively represent HDR photon statistics. Prompts like "Candid street portrait at night in Tokyo" or "Bright living room with sunlight" produce unrealistic tone mapping and washed-out highlights.
  • Physics-Agnostic Reconstruction: Generative priors optimize for natural image manifolds, not photon counting statistics. This means reconstructions can violate physical constraints, producing plausible but photometrically incorrect outputs.
  • Lack of Uncertainty Calibration: Unlike cQBP, which provides explicit error bounds, diffusion-based methods offer no quantification of reconstruction confidence in extreme photon-starved regimes.

These limitations underscore the need for HDR-aware generative models—fine-tuned on tonemapped HDR data or adapted via low-rank techniques—to truly bridge the gap between perceptual quality and physical fidelity in SPAD imaging.
